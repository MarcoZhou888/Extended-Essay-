## 1.1. Introduction
In 2020, the UK economy fell into recession, with GDP falling 25% between February and April.¹ Some sectors were affected more than others, but due to pandemic-induced lockdowns, those with social contact, including hospitality, suffered the most: pubs, restaurants and hotels recorded almost no output in April and May.²

In August 2020, the Eat Out to Help Out scheme was launched by the UK government to encourage consumers to consume restaurant food. The scheme provided consumers with a 50% price reduction (with the government reimbursing restaurants for this price reduction) on all food and non-alcoholic drinks consumed in a participating business from Monday to Wednesday, up to a limit of £10 per person during August. The scheme effectively acted as a subsidy, an amount of money paid by the government to firms per unit of output, on food and non-alcoholic drinks consumed in restaurants.

The focus of this investigation is Kent, a county in South East England. The South East was the region with the second highest number of meal claims in the UK (behind only London), with 12,992,000 meals claimed for. This shows that the scheme was particularly impactful in Kent.

However, as the scheme only ran Monday-Wednesday, it is possible that consumers just shifted consumption earlier in the week, rather than increasing consumption overall. The investigation of the Eat Out to Help Out scheme is therefore significant (it impacted a large number of restaurants), and worthy of investigation (as the size of the impact is unknown) in Kent.

Therefore, the question that will be investigated is "To what extent did the UK government's 'Eat Out to Help Out' scheme increase the consumption of restaurant food in Kent?"

## 1.2. Hypothesis
I predict that the overall level of restaurant food consumption increased as a direct result of the Eat Out to Help Out scheme, as it is likely that the increase in consumption on the days the scheme was running (Monday-Wednesday) outweighed the potential reduced consumption on other days of the week. Nonetheless, this depends heavily on the price elasticity of the demand for restaurant food in the context of the pandemic; the extent to which people were confident enough to go out and eat (both in terms of catching COVID-19 and worries of future financial problems due to job losses) will need to be investigated.

## 2.1. Secondary data on the scheme's impact
Understanding patterns of restaurant food consumption from pre-pandemic, during the Eat Out to Help Out scheme, and after the scheme ended is essential in determining whether consumption increased as a direct result of the scheme. A subsidy is an amount of money paid by the government to firms per unit of output. The scheme effectively acted as a subsidy on restaurant food in the UK, with the explicit objective being to increase consumption, and minimise job losses.

**(Figure 1: Subsidy on Restaurant Food in Kent)**
*Figure 1 shows the market for restaurant foods in Kent, with price on the y-axis and quantity on the x-axis. Initially, the equilibrium is seen where S (Supply) = D (Demand), with Q restaurant food bought and sold at price £P. The government subsidy paid to producers (50% of the price of each restaurant meal, up to £10) would offset some of producers' costs of production, enabling them to reduce the prices charged to consumers. Therefore, S shifts down to S+sub. The market reaches a new equilibrium where S+sub = D, with Q1 restaurant food bought and sold at price £P1. The objective of the scheme can be seen on the diagram: the lower price allows a higher number of people to access restaurant foods. Although Figure 1 cannot accurately depict Kent's restaurant food market (it is a differentiated product, so more expensive restaurant foods will receive bigger subsidies), it conceptually illustrates well the scheme's desired impact.*

UK government data reports a total of 12.9 million meals claimed for under the Eat Out to Help Out scheme in South East England. Though this is not localised to Kent specifically, a large proportion of the South East is Kent. This region had the second highest number of meals claimed, behind only London, and therefore the scheme certainly impacted consumption in the region. However, total claim numbers do not indicate the scheme's success at increasing consumption, because many of these people may have gone to eat out even without the scheme, so consumption must be compared to the time periods immediately before and after August 2020. 

Data from Revolut, a card transaction company, indicates the scheme's impact on restaurant food consumption by showing the proportion of restaurant transactions that fell on each day of the week. With data taken from multiple time periods in-and-around the scheme's implementation, data indicates that the proportion of transactions that fell on Mondays, Tuesdays and Wednesdays was significantly higher during the scheme than in any other time period, as shown by Table 1 below:

**Table 1: Proportion of restaurant transactions on each day of the week**

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

Table 1 shows that the Eat Out to Help Out scheme clearly impacted consumption habits: consumers were more likely to eat out at restaurants on days when they could claim the subsidised price. Nonetheless, the extent of this impact is smaller than I had hypothesised. Moreover, as the data is only a proportion, further research is needed to tell if overall consumption increased or decreased.

Similar data from Barclaycard indicates that spending in restaurants and fast-food chains was up by 34.2% on Monday-to-Wednesday in August compared to July. Surprisingly, it was also 33% up on Thursday-Sunday. However, the total value of transactions was down by 7% from August 2019. This indicates that although overall consumption of restaurant foods is likely to have increased from July (and other lockdown months), the data runs contrary to my hypothesis. Instead of eating patterns shifting from weekends to weekdays, consumption increased across all days of the week, with weekday consumption only increasing by 1.2 percentage points more than weekend consumption. This shows that it is likely that something other than the Eat Out to Help Out scheme was causing the majority of the month-on-month increase in consumption, with the scheme only being responsible for a small part of the increase on weekdays. Therefore, this data suggests that the scheme was not very effective at increasing consumption.

Additionally, this data shows that overall restaurant food consumption did not increase enough to return it to pre-pandemic levels. This assumption is backed up by official data which shows a decrease of £9623 million of spending on restaurants and hotels between quarter 3 in 2019 and quarter 3 in 2020. The significant drop in spending suggests that other factors must have been preventing a substantial increase in consumption, despite the lower price. However, the data includes hotels as well as restaurants, which did not have the same subsidies available to consumers. Furthermore, the data is only by quarter, so the overall impact of the scheme cannot be seen as the scheme only ran in August.

**(Figure 2: Subsidy on Restaurant Food in Kent, with Inelastic PED)**
*Figure 2 repeats Figure 1's analysis, but illustrates the data's indication that the demand for restaurant foods may have been price-inelastic during August 2020, as other factors seem to be limiting the consumption increase, despite the lower price. Demand is price-inelastic when a change in price causes a proportionally smaller change in quantity demanded. As a result, the large decrease in price (P to P1) has caused only a small increase in consumption (Q to Q1): the 50% decrease in price caused a less than 50% increase in restaurant food consumption.*

Instead, the overall increase in restaurant food consumption was likely due to other factors, including an increase in confidence over COVID cases, or an increase in real income following workers returning to full salary after the furlough scheme. Although the total number of claims made using the scheme is high, it is difficult to use secondary data on transactions to indicate how the scheme alone impacted consumer behaviour, because many other factors were changing at the time: the ceteris paribus assumption is lacking when just observing empirical data. This will be addressed in the next part of the essay, which focuses much more sharply on consumer behaviour in Kent, rather than large-scale, UK-wide empirical observations.

Nonetheless, a combination of the Revolut and Barclaycard data does give a strong indication of the scheme's impact. Revolut seems to overestimate the scheme's impact on weekday consumption, which may be because it has a disproportionate number of users from a younger and more metropolitan demographic. With only a few million users in total, sample bias may limit the accuracy of this data in comparison to Barclaycard, which accounts for around 40% of all card transactions. Although not localised to Kent, this allows for a comprehensive representation of consumption patterns, demonstrating the limited impact of the Eat Out to Help Out scheme in comparison to other changing factors at the time. Therefore, secondary data indicates that the scheme definitely impacted consumption and incentivised people to eat out more on Monday to Wednesday, as per my hypothesis. However, the size of this is unclear: data seems to indicate that other factors had a bigger impact on consumption on all days, compared to the scheme's impact on Monday-Wednesday. Furthermore, the scheme's impact was not big enough to return consumption to pre-pandemic levels, indicating a price-inelastic demand.

## 2.2. Impact of the scheme on consumer behaviour
This section aims to sharpen the focus of the scheme's impact by analysing how consumer behaviour changed, because of the Eat Out to Help Out scheme, rather than just analysing how consumer behaviour changed generally in August 2020. This section focuses specifically on consumers who live within Kent.

Data from Opentable, gathered via the House of Commons library, gives a more comprehensive perspective on how the scheme impacted consumer behaviour, with comparisons to both 2019 and July 2020 given. Data is shown by Figure 3 below:

**(Figure 3: Impact of Eat Out to Help Out on Dining Habits - Opentable)**
*Dining out increased on Eat Out to Help Out days. Seated diners at UK restaurants, % change on previous year.*

By giving a week-by-week breakdown, Figure 3 is more illustrative of the scheme's impact on consumer behaviour, as it enables me to isolate general consumption trends during August separately from the scheme's specific impact on certain days. Figure 3 indicates that the number of bookings for eating out increased substantially Monday-to-Wednesday. Consumption in the first week of August is substantially higher than the last week of July 2020, and consumption in the last week of August is substantially higher than September 2020. Even when compared to 2019 (pre-pandemic), consumers were more likely to eat out during the scheme, showing that its impact on consumption may have been more substantial than previously anticipated.

Moreover, the scheme does not seem to have reduced consumption on Thursday-Sunday: consumption in the first week of August (compared to August 2019) is basically the same as the last week of July (compared to July 2019), implying that weekend consumption still followed annual patterns despite the scheme being in place earlier in the week. Consumption across all days increased during August (probably due to a decline in COVID causes and therefore an increase in confidence to go out), but the scheme itself does not seem to have reduced consumption Thursday-Sunday away from its gradual increase. In corroboration with the Barclaycard data, this paints a more compelling picture of the reasons why Thursday-to-Sunday consumption grew by 33% compared to July (because consumption trends were generally growing through August), whereas it is clear that the growth in Monday-to-Wednesday consumption by 34.2% is due to the scheme to a significant extent, over and above just general economic trends. This shows that the scheme did have a stimulating effect on restaurant food consumption, backing up my hypothesis.

The first clear result of this research is that the scheme was well-publicised: 97% of restaurant consumers in Kent knew about the scheme, illustrating its substantial potential impact. Furthermore, people on average were more likely to be encouraged to eat out by the scheme than they were discouraged to eat out on Thursday-Sunday, with the exception of those older than 55, who more substantially reduced their eating out later in the week. This could perhaps be explained by older consumers being more likely to be worried about COVID (also shown in Table 2), negating the scheme's impact on these consumers.

The stimulating effect of the scheme seems to be larger among younger consumers, perhaps because they typically earn less and therefore are more responsive to price changes. This is backed up by younger people being much more likely to be deterred from eating out because it is too expensive. It can therefore be said that, when trying to isolate the impact of the scheme within Kent, the scheme did have a stimulating effect on consumption, which was more pronounced amongst younger consumers who have lower incomes and were less concerned about catching COVID, making demand more price-elastic.

Interestingly, primary survey data indicate a relatively small impact caused by the scheme, despite governmental statistics stating that £849 million was claimed through 160 million meals during its implementation. This may be because many claimants would have eaten out on those days anyway (meaning there was a high number of claims despite consumption not being significantly increased), or due to the fact that the scheme was in place almost two years prior to the survey being conducted, so people misremembered their actions. Furthermore, due to the relatively small sample size, the survey's accuracy may be limited. Alternatively, the scheme may have had less impact on people within Kent than elsewhere in the UK.

A more theoretical investigation of the determinants of the price elasticity of demand (the responsiveness of quantity demanded when there is a change in the price of a product²¹) for restaurant food may provide some insight. One such determinant is income: higher-income earners spend a smaller proportion of their income on restaurant food, making them less responsive to price changes that are less noticeable. For these consumers, the scheme would have had a smaller stimulating impact on consumption.

According to data from Kent Analytics, Kent’s average gross disposable household income (GDHI) is almost £1000 higher than Great Britain's average GDHI.²² This is further supported by data from the Office for National Statistics which shows that Mid and West Kent have a higher average household income than the rest of the UK (5% and 31% respectively), and East Kent and Kent Thames Gateway are only slightly lower than the rest of the UK.²³ Therefore, it is reasonable to assume, due to the multiple sources suggesting the same, that Kent has an overall higher average income than the UK, making demand more price-inelastic and therefore, potentially explaining why the Eat Out to Help Out scheme seems to have been less impactful in Kent than in the UK as a whole.

Overall, the government clearly promoted the scheme effectively, and it had a statistically significant impact on increasing restaurant food consumption on Monday-to-Wednesday, but also overall. However, some data does indicate that the scheme’s effectiveness is diminished in Kent, where the average disposable income is higher than other parts of the UK, making demand less responsive to price changes.

## 2.3. Impact of the scheme on businesses
Analysing the Eat Out to Help Out scheme’s impact from the perspective of local businesses will provide further insight into its impact on restaurant food consumption.

Interviews with restaurant and pub owners conducted by Kent Online suggest that business owners believe the scheme to have been successful, so much so that many continued the 50% discount throughout September, and funded it themselves without government support.²⁴ One pub owner claimed that they had been ‘fully booked all month’ and were at ‘pre-covid capacity’. Another restaurant owner claimed they were ‘busier this month [August] than they were for Christmas last year’. This clearly shows that business owners in Kent believe the scheme to have been a huge success, and consumption of restaurant foods has clearly increased, particularly on days when the scheme was running. However, these interviews were only conducted with a handful of owners, and therefore, despite the overwhelming positive feedback, the sample size is too small to make any final conclusions. Furthermore, Kent Online is a regional news source and therefore may only present positive interviews to promote a positive image of Kent. A more quantitative investigation into the scheme may give a better picture as to whether it was effective or not.

Though, this data was established by looking at daily mobility data from google, which may lead to some inaccuracy if people do not allow google to monitor their location.

Data from the Office for National Statistics shows that vacancies were relatively low during the Eat Out to Help Out scheme’s duration. Nonetheless, this low number may have been because most restaurant staff were retained on furlough during lockdown.

To further this knowledge I conducted an interview with an employee of a local pub-restaurant in Kent - The Ship Inn at Southfleet, who worked for the duration of the scheme, and beyond. She claimed that the pub was ‘packed’ through Monday-Wednesday in August, though the weekend was significantly quieter than usual. The scheme clearly had a stimulating impact on her place of work as the scheme was continued into September, self-funded. Though this may seem to back up the claim that the scheme stimulated the hospitality sector, this interview was based on a personal experience, and was a recall of an event that took place almost two years ago, which means there could be inaccuracies. Furthermore, this is only the view of one, small pub in Kent.

Nonetheless, despite potential inaccuracies with the data, it is reasonable to assume that restaurants within Kent benefited greatly from the scheme, and the noticeable impact on consumption on Monday-to-Wednesday indicates that consumption substantially increased, to the point where many restaurants have continued to fund a similar scheme themselves.

# 3. Conclusion

In conclusion, my hypothesis was somewhat correct: it is clear that the consumption of restaurant food increased in August 2020; however, this was caused by other factors related to the pandemic, as well as the government’s ‘Eat Out to Help Out’ scheme. The scheme certainly did have some stimulating impact on consumption, but not enough to match pre-pandemic levels. Furthermore, the effect of the scheme on increasing consumption was dependent on consumer income, with the effect being largest on lower-income, younger consumers in Kent. Through the government’s effective promotion of the scheme, businesses were positively impacted, and many saw more customers than ever. This shows the success of the scheme: the restaurant sector was re-stimulated following lockdowns.

Although it can be argued that the scheme was beneficial in increasing restaurant food consumption, the scheme was extremely expensive for the UK government, coming at an opportunity cost of funding for other industries that were in need. The long-term impact of government debt against the short-term benefits of the scheme on the restaurant sector is a question that arises from this research, that more data would be needed to ascertain.
