## 3. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Characteristics of the oligopolistic food delivery market
In economic theory, an oligopoly is characterized by a few large firms that are highly interdependent and possess significant market shares[cite: 1590]. [cite_start]Firms in oligopolies face substantial barriers to entry, impediments that prevent rival firms from entering the market easily, and could produce either homogeneous or differentiated products[cite: 1591].

1. **High start-up costs:** The technological costs of building up food delivery companies are substantial, with foodpanda requiring $20 million of initial funding (Kumar, 2021)[cite: 1592]. [cite_start]These significant start-up costs represent high barriers to entry, posing a challenge for new entrants to raise the necessary capital to enter the market[cite: 1593]. [cite_start]High start-up costs go towards the development of a platform website or app, acquiring restaurant-partners and hiring a delivery fleet[cite: 1594].
2.**Homogeneity of products:** Homogenous products are highly substitutable, while differentiated products appeal to different consumer groups and confer more pricing power on firms[cite: 1595]. [cite_start]While there is a high degree of substitutability between the delivery service of the various platforms, the companies attempt to differentiate in terms of service and restaurants offered, in an attempt to develop consumer loyalty and raise demand and profits over time[cite: 1596].
3.**Strategic interdependence of firms:** Oligopolistic firms are interdependent, engaging in strategic behaviour that takes into account the possible action of their rivals, illustrated by the game theory model of oligopolistic behavior[cite: 1597]. [cite_start]The firms account for price cuts and match products of rival firms through the similar pricing of the food items across platforms[cite: 1598].

Oligopolistic firms typically seek to avoid price wars as rival firms are likely to match price cuts, leading to lower revenues and profits for the firms[cite: 1599]. [cite_start]This EE will assess the extent that the oligopolistic firms in the food delivery service engage in price and non-price competition[cite: 1600].

### 3.2 Concentration Ratio
Concentration ratio is an indicator to identify the market structure of an industry[cite: 1601]. [cite_start]In theory, the degree of competition in an industry between firms indicates the market share of the largest firms[cite: 1602]. [cite_start]The higher the concentration ratio, the lower the degree of competition between the firms, vice versa[cite: 1603].

The market shares of the firms are not publicly available and hence it is difficult to directly determine the concentration ratio of the firms in the industry[cite: 1604]. [cite_start]However, using a combination of survey results and other market information, we can deduce the extent of dominance of these three firms in Singapore[cite: 1605]. 

The market value of firms are highly correlated with their market shares and their revenues[cite: 1606]. [cite_start]Given that GrabFood, deliveroo and foodpanda are valued at US$ 14.3 billion, US$ 7 billion and US$ 3 billion respectively, they represent the three largest firms in the food delivery market in Singapore that comprises a total of six firms including Oddle, WhyQ and AirAsia[cite: 1607]. 
Furthermore, according to a survey by Rakuten Insight of more than 5,000 consumers in Singapore, approximately 77% of respondents indicated GrabFood as their most used app for ordering food in 2020[cite: 1608]. [cite_start]GrabFood, together with deliveroo, had reportedly cornered 60% of the food delivery market in Singapore by 2019 (Wirtz, 2021)[cite: 1608]. [cite_start]Hence, it is evident that the firms are operating in an oligopolistic market structure[cite: 1609].

## 4. AN OLIGOPOLISTIC FOOD DELIVERY INDUSTRY

### 4.1 Price Competition
In theory, price competition is used to gain market share by incentivizing consumers to increase quantity demanded in response to lower prices[cite: 1610]. [cite_start]However, in an oligopolistic industry, price competition often results in price wars due to the interdependence of firms that lead to swift retaliation by rival firms to defend their market share[cite: 1611]. [cite_start]It is assumed that all firms are profit maximising, where the quantity of output produced is where the Marginal Revenue (MR) curve equals the Marginal Cost (MC) curve (MR=MC)[cite: 1612]. [cite_start]While a decrease in price will lead to an increase in market share, price wars do not benefit any firm as lower prices lead to lower revenues and profits for firms involved[cite: 1613].

Primary research was undertaken to establish the actual competitive behaviour of the firms to evaluate the extent that they engage in price and non-price competition[cite: 1614]. [cite_start]Through consumer surveys and primary analysis on the pricing of various food items on the delivery platforms, there is little evidence of extensive price competition among firms[cite: 1615]. [cite_start]This price rigidity observed can be justified through the kinked demand curve elaborated below[cite: 1616].
In theory, the kinked demand curve suggests price stability amongst competitive oligopolies[cite: 1617]. [cite_start]Given the undesirable outcomes of price competition between firms, there is often price rigidity in an oligopolistic market with firms unwilling to deviate from the market range of prices[cite: 1618]. [cite_start]This price rigidity can be illustrated through the kinked D-curve[cite: 1619].

**Fig 1.1: Illustration of the kinked demand curve**
Each firm perceives their demand curve to be elastic for prices above P1 and inelastic for prices below P1[cite: 1620]. [cite_start]If an oligopolist lowers price, the rivals will follow suit to defend their market share (Fig 1.1)[cite: 1621]. [cite_start]The fall in price leads to less than proportionate rise in Q4 thus, both firms retain their market share but gain a lower sales revenue[cite: 1622]. [cite_start]As a result, the firms are reluctant to reduce prices[cite: 1623]. [cite_start]However, if an oligopolist raises its price, rivals will not follow suit as consumers switch to cheaper substitutes offered by rivals, resulting in a more than proportionate fall in Qd and lower revenues and profits for the firm[cite: 1624]. [cite_start]The non-colluding firms' prices and output decisions are consistent with the MR-MC profit-maximising rule which accounts for the kinked portion of the MR curve such that the firms produce at Q1 and set their prices at P1[cite: 1625].

Firstly, from research gathered, while there are clear differences in the various components of what comprises a delivery company's final delivery cost (delivery fees, small order fee, service fee), there is a high degree of consistency in terms of total fees charged across the online platforms[cite: 1625]. [cite_start]There is little evidence of price competition, as no firm actively seeks to undercut the pricing of their rivals[cite: 1626]. [cite_start]GrabFood is observed to have the cheapest delivery fee across all three comparisons while foodpanda has the lowest or no small order fee[cite: 1627]. 

For example, the total meal cost payable by consumers for the low-priced item across the platforms averaged $13.26[cite: 1628]. [cite_start]However, a closer look at the pricing reveals that deliveroo tends to be a more expensive option with a total cost of $16.19 as compared to GrabFood and foodpanda for small orders at $13.10 and $10.49 respectively[cite: 1629]. [cite_start]This could be down to cost differences or the competitive positioning of the firm rather than GrabFood and foodpandas' attempt at price competition[cite: 1630].

Furthermore, the consumer survey revealed that consumers are unaware of any significant price differences across platforms (Fig 1.2)[cite: 1631]. [cite_start]This can be partially explained by imperfect information on the part of the customer due to the lack of a convenient way to compare prices which supports the assertion that price competition is absent among such platforms[cite: 1632]. [cite_start]If firms were engaging in price competition, it is imperative that consumers are aware of the lowest-price producer for the firm to increase its market share at the expense of rival firms[cite: 1633]

### 4.2 Non-price competition
[cite_start]Due to the limitations of price competition, in theory, oligopolists prefer to engage in non-price competition such as product differentiation as they are unlikely to face immediate retaliation from other firms given that time is required to innovate and establish a differentiated product or service[cite: 1665]. [cite_start]Non-price competition is used to attract consumers by appealing to their tastes and preferences, raising demand, revenues and profits over time[cite: 1666]. [cite_start]The large pool of financial resources in the food delivery industry allows firms to engage in Research and Development (R&D) or advertising to increase their market share[cite: 1667].

[cite_start]Research conducted revealed a significant degree of non-price competition between the firms[cite: 1668]. [cite_start]The behaviors of the firms can once again be predicted by game theory as oligopolists attempt to match the advertisements offered by rival firms to entice consumers[cite: 1669]. [cite_start]Given the aim of firms which is to profit maximise, advertising is likely to be the preferred strategy as it requires lower costs, allowing the firms to decrease their total costs and increase profits[cite: 1670]. [cite_start]However, through research it can be seen that the firms engage in strong advertising which is likely to be the dominant strategy as it can influence consumer decisions thus likely to increase the market share of the firms[cite: 1671].

[cite_start]Through observation of public spaces and advertising channels, there is significant evidence of substantial advertising among the delivery platforms, including print and social media advertisements (Fig 1.5)[cite: 1672]. [cite_start]Delivery platforms advertise to associate their services to particular cuisines and restaurants to develop their brand image and differentiate from rivals[cite: 1673]. 

[cite_start]For example, deliveroo focuses on the higher-end market, targeting office workers in the Central Business District (CBD) and wealthier households living in Central Singapore[cite: 1674]. [cite_start]Consequently, their advertisements are more centrally located and promote international cuisines such as pizza (Fig 1.6)[cite: 1675]. [cite_start]On the other hand, foodpanda attempts to position itself as a more 'heartland brand, advertising in train stations and focuses on educating a less tech-savvy clientele about the convenience of food delivery (Fig 1.6)[cite: 1676]. [cite_start]Finally, GrabFood adopts a multi-channel marketing approach, in line with their position as a 'super-app' that provides consumers with a plethora of services on their platform such as ride hailing services and the booking of movie tickets (Fig 1.5)[cite: 1677].

## 5. CONCLUSION
[cite_start]The research suggests that overall, food delivery companies engage predominantly in non-price competition[cite: 1704]. [cite_start]The three largest companies in Singapore engage in minimal price competition while spending heavily on product differentiation and advertising, avoiding the mutually destructive effects of price wars and behaving consistently with the kinked demand curve theory[cite: 1705].

[cite_start]Nonetheless, it is worth considering why the oligopolistic firms choose to engage in non-price competition[cite: 1706]. [cite_start]Firstly, only consumers benefit when firms engage in a price war[cite: 1707]. [cite_start]For example, with regards to delivery fees ranging between $3-$5 per order, the profit margins are already very low[cite: 1708]. [cite_start]Thus, firms cannot withstand prolonged price wars as it will worsen losses and lead to long run shutdown when a firms' revenue falls below the average total costs incurred by the firm[cite: 1709].
